Research

My doctoral work sits at the intersection of classical analysis and computational mathematics, supervised by Prof. Miao-Jung Yvonne Ou at the University of Delaware.

Research Interests

∂

Homogenization Theory

Deriving effective macroscopic models from fine-scale periodic structures in elliptic PDEs — capturing essential physics without resolving every microscopic detail.

λ

Asymptotic Spectral Analysis

Spectral analysis of elliptic PDEs with periodic boundary conditions, with a focus on asymptotic behavior as the period parameter tends to zero.

∇

Multiscale Methods

Building reduced-order models for systems where direct full-scale simulation is prohibitively expensive or impractical across all relevant length scales.

⊕

Scientific Machine Learning

Combining classical homogenization theory with generative models to reconstruct fine-scale structures, improve reduced-order models, and develop efficient tools for real-world multiscale problems.

Σ

Epidemiological Modelling

Earlier work on deterministic compartmental models, sensitivity analysis, and reproduction numbers for infectious disease spread.

Current PhD Focus

My dissertation investigates the asymptotic spectral properties of elliptic PDEs with periodic coefficients. When the period parameter ε → 0, the operator's spectrum exhibits rich structure that classical homogenization theory alone does not fully capture. I use tools from asymptotic analysis, functional analysis, and operator theory to characterize these spectral gaps and limiting behavior.

A longer-term goal is to leverage insights from this spectral theory to guide data-driven reconstruction of fine-scale material properties — bridging the gap between classical PDE analysis and modern machine learning approaches.
